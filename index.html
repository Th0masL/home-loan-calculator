📖 Plain-English glossary

Every term on this page, explained simply — with small examples. Numbers shown are just illustrations.

Getting started

Scenario

A saved "what-if" version of the loan for the same home and income — usually a different down payment. The three starting scenarios are common Estonian choices: KredEx/EIS (10%) = the smallest down payment, using the state guarantee; Standard (15%) = the normal minimum down payment; Larger down (20%) = more cash down for a smaller loan and lower payments. Compare them in the Scenario comparison tab; you can add up to 8, clone, or rename them.

Property price (deal amount)

The full price of the home you want to buy.

Down payment

The cash you pay upfront from your own savings. The bank lends the rest. E.g. on a €200,000 home with a 15% down payment you pay €30,000 yourself and borrow €170,000.

Loan amount (principal)

The money the bank actually lends you — the price minus your down payment. This is what you pay back, plus interest.

Loan term

How many years you have to repay the loan. A longer term means smaller monthly payments but more total interest.

Monthly payment

The fixed amount you pay the bank every month.

How the interest works

Interest rate

The yearly cost of borrowing, as a percentage of what you still owe.

Euribor (benchmark rate)

A Europe-wide "base" interest rate that banks build their loans on. It rises and falls over time. "6-month Euribor" is the version that resets every 6 months.

Bank margin

The bank's fixed mark-up added on top of Euribor. It's set in your contract and doesn't change over the life of the loan.

Contract rate

What you actually pay = Euribor + margin. E.g. 2.4% Euribor + 1.8% margin = 4.2%.

Fixed vs floating rate

With a floating rate your payment changes when Euribor moves. With a fixed rate it's locked for a period — usually a little more expensive, but predictable. This tool holds one rate constant; to compare, just type each rate in turn.

The payment schedule

Annuity

The most common loan type — you pay the same total amount every month for the whole term.

Principal vs interest

Each monthly payment splits in two. The interest part is the bank's fee for that month; the principal part actually reduces your debt. Early on most of the payment is interest; later it's mostly principal.

Amortization schedule

The month-by-month table showing how your loan shrinks to zero.

Balance

How much you still owe. The "opening balance" is what you owe at the start of a given month.

Total interest

All the interest you'll pay across the whole loan — the true extra cost of borrowing, on top of the price.

Can you get the loan? (bank rules)

LTV — Loan-to-Value

Your loan as a percentage of the property price. Lower is safer for the bank. E.g. borrow €170,000 on a €200,000 home = 85% LTV. Estonia usually caps this at 85% (so you need at least 15% down).

DSTI — Debt Service-to-Income

How much of your monthly take-home income goes to loan payments. E.g. €840 of payments on €2,800 income = 30%. Estonia caps this at 50%.

Stress test (stress rate / stress monthly)

A safety check: the bank confirms you could still afford the loan if interest rates jumped. Estonia tests you at 6% (or your rate, whichever is higher), even if your real rate is lower today. "Stress monthly" is that higher what-if payment.

State guarantee (KredEx / EIS in Estonia)

A government-backed scheme that lets eligible buyers borrow with a smaller down payment (e.g. 10% instead of 15%).

Paying it off your way

Repayment holiday

A period at the very start where you pay only the interest, not the principal. Your monthly payment is lower for a while, but the loan costs more in total because the debt isn't shrinking yet.

Extra repayment (prepayment)

Putting extra money toward the loan to pay it off faster and save on interest.

Shorten term vs reduce payment

Two ways to use an extra repayment. Shorten term: keep paying the same monthly amount but finish the loan earlier. Reduce payment: keep the same end date but lower your monthly payment.

Early repayment fee

A charge some banks apply when you pay extra ahead of schedule (e.g. 1% of the extra amount).

Keep or sell your apartment

Appreciation

How much a property gains in value per year. E.g. 3%/year turns a €120,000 flat into about €139,000 in 5 years.

Net monthly rent profit

The rent you collect minus all the costs of owning and renting it out — you enter the final net figure.

Keep advantage

The bottom line of the keep-vs-sell comparison: how much better off you'd be keeping the apartment (rent + value growth) versus selling it to put more cash down on the new home (which means less interest and less remaining debt). Positive = keeping wins; negative = selling wins.

Break-even rent / appreciation

The minimum monthly rent, or yearly value growth, the apartment would need for keeping it to be worthwhile.

Upfront costs

One-time costs

Fees paid once when buying — notary, state fee, property appraisal, and home insurance. They add to the cash you need on day one, on top of the down payment.
